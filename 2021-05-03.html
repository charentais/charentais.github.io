Fruits and Vegetables


2021 : May : 03
How fruit will save us
Ribston Pippin

In July 2018 the drought in the UK affected fruit and vegetable crops. The price of wheat was expected to rise and some vegetables too, due to lower yields. I found that the annual vegetables suffered the most from the prolonged drought.

Vegetables
The carrots, beetroot, kohl rabi, swede, lettuce, tomato and courgettes didn’t do very well. The kale, purple sprouting broccoli and dwarf french beans did okay where I was able to provide more water. The water butt in my garden dried up. There were just too many plants in pots and in the ground to water consistently.

Fruits
Some of the fruits on the other hand did very well. The strawberries and summer raspberries weren’t very prolific but the cherries, redcurrants and blackcurrants were fantastic. I have a morello cherry tree in the east facing front garden and I picked a lot of fruit over a few weeks from this tree. There was also a good set of apples, pears and plums that year. A bit of rain later in the season helped to bulk them up. Grape vines and figs also did well. It was an excellent year for commercial grape producers and 2018 was expected to be an exceptional vintage.

Annual vs Perennial
Annual vegetables just wither and die and are so much more work. Perennial fruit bushes and fruit trees have a much deeper root system to help the plants ride out the long period of drought. Annual vegetables have to be sown each year while fruit trees and fruit bushes can last for many years.

Fruit will save us
Fruit can save us when weather systems become more extreme. Grow more fruit, eat more fruit.
