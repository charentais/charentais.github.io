Melons and how to fix melon belly

Melons are a magical fruit. Every melon tastes different and sometimes they taste divine. Melons belong in the cucurbit family and are delicious, especially in the heat of summer. I love melons. All types. My favourite is possibly Cantaloupe, but Honeydew, Piel de Sapo, Galia and the mighty Watermelon are all fantastic. There are more variations appearing in the shops now. There is now a Snowball melon with a very sweet flesh and a citrus like cantaloupe whose name escapes me. If you grow your own then there are many more varieties to choose from. Last summer I tasted Charentais which is a type of Cantaloupe with a delicate flavour. Check out the Charentais melon festival in Provence, France.

Cantaloupe A variety of muskmelon. Orange flesh with a perfume aroma and sweet taste.

Galia A hybrid of cantaloupe and honeydew. Fantastic in season when ripe and soft. Prone to develop mould.

Honeydew Also a variety of muskmelon. Sweet yellow flesh. I like to pick ones with a dark yellow skin and a bit of give. Hard pale yellow fruits tend to be under ripe. Honeydew’s store well. Sometimes they taste savoury. Not sure why and I always feel cheated when I get a savoury honeydew.

Piel de Sapo Also known as Christmas melon or Santa Claus melon. Green skin, pale yellow flesh. Moderately sweet.

Snowball melon A new variety. Off white outer skin with green flecks. Super sweet white flesh.

Watermelon The biggest melon of all. A good one can be very good. I look for a vibrant, firm melon with a little give. A good sun spot on the rind is also worth finding. Also check the stem to see if it is brown. This indicates it was picked when ripe. If you hit unlucky and get a lemon then you’re left with the option of juicing which could save the day. Well at least you don’t feel totally ripped off. Listen to the watermelon pop when you cut it in two. I have noticed towards the end of the European watermelon season that some almost crack open and bust their insides out.

Melon belly, aargh!
I can eat melons but I have to go careful with the quantity. It is also a good idea to eat melon slowly. The general advice is to eat melons alone or leave them alone. I usually eat a melon at the beginning of the day and add other fruits later. The big problem with eating melons is the dreaded melon belly which can make you feel pretty uncomfortable in the gut. It sometimes hits if eating too much or too fast. Some say it is due to a chromium deficiency. Chromium plant foods include broccoli, barley, oats, grapes and potatoes. I don’t know about chromium deficiency and I have never taken a chromium supplement. If melon belly strikes I have a remedy… ginger. If you find yourself struck down with melon belly reach for fresh ginger root. Cut about half a thumb size piece of fresh ginger root and chew the whole piece well. Your melon belly should recede in 5 to 10 minutes. Ginger is great for gut issues although it doesn’t strictly adhere to natural hygiene rules. May be worth a try if you don’t make a habit out of it. If you get your digestion really clean then you may not suffer from melon belly. So if you get knocked down on the beach with sand between your teeth and the dreaded melon belly try ginger root.
