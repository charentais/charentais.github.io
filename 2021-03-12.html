Crown grafting an apple tree

14 April
I have a cooking apple called Dummelow’s Seedling. I obtained the scion wood from The National Fruit Collection in Kent after reading good reviews about it and if you like Cooking apples then it may be a very good choice. I prefer to eat my apples raw at and the Dummelow’s is far too acidic almost up to the end of the season. When the acid dissipates the apples have no taste. Dummelow’s Seedling originated at Shackerstone in Leicestershire. It was grown by Richard Dumeller. It was a widely grown cooking apple in Victorian England. It is acidic and keeps well into the following year, even up until May.

I have read about producing one apple tree with many different varieties on the branches and also about changing the cultivar from one variety to another. I prefer the second method as the first seems a bit of a gimmick. I have plenty of experience grafting apple scions onto apple rootstocks so I thought, I can do this!

It was a shame cutting off all the growth of the Dummelow’s as the buds were starting to swell and I would also be losing a variety of apple. The tree isn’t in the best spot and it is trying to grow towards the sunlight.

I got hold of some scion wood from a local dessert apple tree (variety unknown) which ripens fairly early in the season. I took the scion wood in early April. The recommended time for collecting scion wood for grafting is mid winter. The scion wood is kept in the fridge until grafting time. The idea being that the scion wood should be dormant while the rootstock should be springing into life. If the scion wood doesn’t take then there is plenty of wood on the Dummelow’s left to try again next year.

Equipment: grafting knife; pruning saw; secateurs; sealing wax; graft wood; disinfectant.

I sterilised all the equipment coming into contact with the tree using a weak solution of Dettol or similar.

So after sawing off all the branches of the old tree with a pruning saw I cut across the main stem, about 90cm from the ground. I then used a grafting knife and made a 30-40mm vertical cut into the bark of the tree approximately 3mm deep. This cuts into the outer bark and the inner layer. It is quite easy to see the distinct layer next to the outer bark.

On the scion wood I made a diagonal cut just as in doing a whip and tongue graft but without the tongue, sloping down to a point at the end. This scion wood is then inserted underneath the bark of the tree with the cut scion wood facing inwards. I did 3 grafts spaced equally apart hoping that at least one will take.

When all the grafts were in place I then wrapped round using insulation tape to hold and seal the grafts. The final step was to seal the top of the exposed wood with grafting wax. I used Tenax grafting wax heated up by placing the tub in a hot water bath. I applied the wax with a putty knife to seal all the bare wood.

Done. I now have to wait to see if any of the grafts take which will hopefully be within 6 - 8 weeks.

Update
At the end of May it seemed like the grafts had not taken, as there had been some early growth that then stopped and the leaves appeared to be dying back. But in early June I noticed the leaves on the scions taking off and by the end of June the scions appear to be growing well.

Mid July and the scions are growing well so it’s time to remove the tape. The grafts seem to have taken so all is good. After cutting the tree back to the main trunk a lot of new shoots have appeared. These will have to be removed so only the new grafts are growing.
